# Smoke test — scientific-writing skill

Functional near-infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS) is a non-invasive optical neuroimaging modality that measures task-evoked changes in cortical hemodynamics by quantifying oxygenated and deoxygenated hemoglobin via the modified Beer–Lambert law [REF1]. Unlike functional magnetic resonance imaging, fNIRS tolerates participant motion, operates in naturalistic settings, and offers a favorable trade-off between spatial specificity and temporal resolution at comparatively low cost [REF2]. These properties have driven its expanding use across cognitive, developmental, and clinical research, including affective and anxiety disorders, where ecologically valid measurement of prefrontal activation is increasingly emphasized [REF3]. Together, these strengths position fNIRS as a practical platform for biomarker discovery in mental health.
